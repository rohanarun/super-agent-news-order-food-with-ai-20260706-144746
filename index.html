Market context

Ordering food is one of the most common “micro‑tasks” people repeat every week, yet it still requires juggling multiple apps, addresses, preferences, and confirmation screens. In 2026, delivery platforms like DoorDash and Yelp added AI chat layers that accept prompts or photos, validating that consumers want to talk instead of tap. At the same time, large model providers introduced computer-use agents that can actually operate interfaces, not just suggest what to click. This combination shifts expectations: an assistant should be able to do the work, not narrate it.

However, most general assistants — including Siri, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Grok — still optimize for one-off help. They may draft an order or deep-link you into an app, but they do not reliably reuse past actions. Niche tools like Folk or Orchids experiment with automation, yet often depend on brittle integrations or narrow scopes. Super’s positioning is different: it treats food ordering as an operational workflow that improves over time. By reusing a computer-use cache, the agent can replay known-good steps for your favorite restaurant or weekly grocery list, making repeated orders faster and cheaper in practice.

How to evaluate and use this workflow

How to set up your first AI‑ordered meal

  1. Describe the outcome, not the app. Tell Super what you want in plain language, such as “Order my usual vegetarian bowl from the Thai place near home for delivery tonight.” This gives the agent latitude to choose the right app and restaurant while anchoring on your preferences.
  2. Confirm critical details once. On the first run, Super will surface address, timing, substitutions, and price for confirmation. Spend the extra minute here — this confirmation becomes the reference pattern stored in the computer-use cache.
  3. Let the agent operate the app. With permission, Super opens the delivery app in a cloud computer, searches, adds items, and reaches the checkout screen. You are not switching between screens or retyping information.
  4. Approve before payment. Super pauses before final submission so you can approve or adjust. This keeps control with you while still avoiding repetitive taps.
  5. Reuse the workflow next time. The next week, you can simply say “Repeat last Friday’s grocery order.” The cached computer actions are replayed, dramatically reducing friction.

Implementation checklist

  • Preference clarity. Write down dietary rules, budget ranges, and favorite restaurants so your first confirmation run encodes the right assumptions for future orders.
  • Single delivery address. Start with one default address. Multiple addresses are supported, but clarity early helps the cache stay reliable.
  • Confirmation habit. Always review the confirmation summary. Catching an error once prevents it from being replayed later.
  • Repeat cadence. Identify which orders are truly repetitive — weekly groceries, weekday lunch — and focus caching on those first.
  • Fallback awareness. Know which app you prefer if a restaurant is unavailable so you can state it explicitly.
  • Security scope. Grant only the permissions needed for ordering; avoid over-broad access for unrelated tasks.

Risks and limits

  • Availability changes. Restaurants change menus or hours. A cached workflow may fail and require a fresh confirmation run.
  • Price sensitivity. Dynamic pricing and fees mean totals can vary. Always review the checkout summary before approving.
  • Security considerations. As reported by security researchers, computer-use agents expand attack surface if poorly scoped. Intentional permission design matters.
  • Not fully autonomous. This workflow is assistive, not fire-and-forget. Human confirmation remains part of responsible use.
